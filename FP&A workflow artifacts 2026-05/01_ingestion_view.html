FP&A_Close_SOP_v3.2.docx — page 14

A representative page from the procedural manual. The system extracts atomic procedural rules (highlighted) and links them to the relevant process step.

Source excerpt

3.2 Regional forecast consolidation

Each regional finance lead submits their forecast workbook to the shared Forecasts/2026/Mx/ folder by EOD on the 4th business day of the close cycle. The corporate FP&A analyst is responsible for verifying the submission against the standard template before consolidating.

If the regional total in cell D60 differs from the sum of the line items on the Detail tab by more than $1,000, the workbook must be returned to the regional lead with a variance log. Do not overwrite the regional submission — append corrections to the Adjustments tab so the audit trail is preserved.

FX translation is performed using the 3-month forward curve as of the last business day of the prior month, sourced from the FX Rate Loader workbook (cf. §4.1).

Extracted facts

  • Trigger: Regional submission, due BD+4 17:00 local
  • Validation rule: |D60 − sum(Detail)| ≤ $1,000 → else return with variance log
  • Mutation rule: Never overwrite — append to Adjustments tab
  • FX policy: 3-month forward as of last BD of prior month
  • Cross-reference: §4.1 (FX Rate Loader)

Interview with J. Park, Sr. Analyst — turn 23

The agent is interviewing the senior analyst to clarify a pattern observed in the recordings: how she decides when a forecast variance is "real" vs. a coding issue.

Conversation

Discovery agent
In the March recording, you spent ~6 minutes investigating an AMER variance of about $4.2k. The SOP says any variance above $1,000 triggers a return-to-region. But in the recording you didn't return it — you fixed it yourself. Can you walk me through how you decided?
J. Park
Yeah, the SOP rule is right in spirit but in practice if it's clearly a SKU mapping issue and I can fix it in <10 minutes, I just do it and tell them. Returning the workbook costs us a day because they're in different time zones. If it's a real volume or pricing change I send it back.
Discovery agent
So the actual rule is closer to: if the variance is a known coding pattern (miscoded SKU, channel mismatch, currency tag), correct in place; otherwise return. Does that match?
J. Park
Exactly. There's like 4 or 5 patterns that account for ~80% of variances. If it's one of those, I fix it. If it's something I haven't seen before, I escalate.
Discovery agent
Got it. I'll model that as: auto-classify variance → if known pattern, propose correction for human approval; else route to regional lead. The 4–5 patterns become the agent's first-pass triage. Does anyone else apply different rules here?
J. Park
M. Chen sometimes overrides me on the channel mismatches — she'll want to see them either way for trend analysis. So channel mismatches probably should always escalate even if I could fix them.

What the system synthesized

  • Refined variance rule: triage by pattern — auto-correct for SKU mapping, channel mismatch (now flagged "always escalate per M. Chen"), currency tag, calendar-shift.
  • Stakeholder-specific override captured: channel mismatches always visible to VP FP&A.
  • Latency rationale recorded: regional time zones make return-to-region a 1-day cost — informs how the agent weighs auto-fix vs. escalate.
  • Open question for next interview: are there other "stakeholder overrides" we should capture?
