The 5-Layer Platform Stack

How "automating the automation" decomposes into a stack of governable, reviewable layers — and where the moat lives versus the table-stakes.

The wedge isn't automating workflows — Zapier and Workato already do that. It's automating the modeling of workflows, which today requires a $300/hr consultant to interview ten people for a month before anyone can write the first Zap. The five layers below stack like a dependency chain: each layer's output is a structured artifact the layer above consumes, and a human can audit and edit at every level.
Section 1

The stack — bottom to top

Layer 5

Agents

Zapier: ✗

Specialized agents instantiated for the judgment-laden steps that deterministic RPA can't reach.

Inputs

  • Process model from Layer 3
  • Ontology from Layer 2
  • Integrations from Layer 4
  • Per-task eval set

Output (artifact)

  • Agent specs that read like job descriptions: prompt, tools, eval set, escalation policy, audit-trail schema
  • Running instances with HITL hooks

Why this matters

The spec — not the model — is the deliverable. A spec is reviewable, governable, and survives model upgrades. Without specs, agents are just opaque LLM calls.

Layer 4

Integrations

Zapier: ✓

Authenticated, observable connections to the source systems the process depends on.

← Zapier-class tools live here. Plus a hand-written workflow builder that assumes Layers 1–3 already happened in someone's head.

Inputs

  • Process model's required endpoints
  • Customer credentials (OAuth, API keys)
  • Schema mappings from Layer 2

Output (artifact)

  • Connector library — typed, observable, rate-limited
  • Per-connector health + lineage telemetry

Why this matters

This is the table-stakes layer — necessary but commodity. A defensible business is not built here; it's built on the layers below and above.

Zapier-class tools sit here. They assume Layers 1–3 already happened in someone's head, and they have nothing structural for Layer 5.

Layer 3

Process

Zapier: ✗

Versioned process model — both as-is and to-be — with named human approvers gating every state transition.

Inputs

  • Ontology from Layer 2
  • Evidence graph from Layer 1
  • Customer human-in-the-loop validation

Output (artifact)

  • Process model with both as-is and to-be states
  • Diff between them, signed off by named humans
  • Validations / controls (the "C-01..C-07" pattern)

Why this matters

Customers sign off on what gets automated before it goes live, not after. HITL transitions are how trust is earned. This is where SOX/J-SOX defensibility lives.

Layer 2

Ontology

Zapier: ✗

Shared vocabulary that turns observed facts into something more than one customer's transcript — entities, relationships, controls, role mappings.

Inputs

  • Evidence graph from Layer 1
  • Domain templates (FP&A, P2P, OTC, ITGC...)
  • Customer-specific overrides via HITL

Output (artifact)

  • Typed schema: entity classes, relationships, controls, validations, role & system mappings
  • Confidence per assertion

Why this matters

This is the strategic moat. Without ontology, discovery is a transcript and agents are point solutions. With it, "this customer's variance triage rule" and "that customer's expense override" become the same shape of thing — a platform, not consulting.

Layer 1

Discovery

Zapier: ✗

Multi-modal process mining — ingests four kinds of evidence to build a factual claim about how work happens today.

Inputs

  • Word / PDF manuals & SOPs
  • Excel workbooks + VBA macros
  • Screen recordings of analysts
  • Structured AI-led interviews

Output (artifact)

  • Evidence graph — atomic procedural facts, each linked to its source
  • Confidence score per claim
  • Surfaced gaps & conflicts

Why this matters

The wedge that breaks the consulting bottleneck. The defensible bar: surface what the customer can not articulate — the undocumented exceptions and the informal escalations nobody put in the SOP.

Section 2

The first-class artifact principle

Each layer emits a structured, reviewable, governable artifact.

No layer hands an opaque blob to the next. This is what makes the platform defensible against "GPT can do that" — because GPT is the engine inside Layer 5, but Layers 1–4 produce inputs an off-the-shelf model alone cannot.

Section 3

Moat & risk

The moat — Layer 2 (Ontology)

Why this layer in particular is defensible.

Without ontology, discovery is a transcript and agents are point solutions. With it, the platform recognizes that disparate customer rules are the same shape of thing — variance triage, policy override, exception escalation — and that's what makes it a platform instead of a per-customer consulting engagement.

Domain templates (FP&A, P2P, OTC, ITGC...) solve the cold start. Learning customer-specific specializations from observed evidence is the long-term defense.

The risk — and how we win it

The most likely attack vector from incumbents.

An incumbent — Zapier, ServiceNow, or someone newer — bolts on a Layer 5 with off-the-shelf LLMs and tells the customer that Layers 1–3 are "your problem to scope." That commoditizes us before we've established the moat.

We win by making Discovery surface what the customer cannot articulate: not just the workflow they described in the kickoff, but the four undocumented exceptions and the informal escalation that never made it into the SOP. That is the demo's "M. Chen always-escalate channel-mismatches" beat — credible only if the discovery → ontology → process pipeline genuinely sees what the customer cannot.
